Human Needs

Maslow's Hierarchy

Lower, less complex needs must be satisfied before considering higher, more complex needs.

  • Physiological
  • Safety and Security
  • Belonging
    • give and receive friendship and love
  • Esteem
    • Internal - self-esteem, confidence, independence
    • External - recognition, reputation, status
  • Cognitive and Aesthetic - understand the world around you
    • Cognitive - knowledge, understanding
    • Aesthetic - opinions, tastes (can affect instructor / learner relationship)
  • Self-Actualization
    • fulfill potential
    • solve problems
    • concerned about personal growth
    • appreciate life

Teaching Adult Learners

Adult Learners:

  • seek new knowledge or skills as a means to an end, not an end in itself
  • can be coping with specific life-changing events: marriage, divorce, a new job
  • are autonomous, self-directed, independent, and want to exercise control
  • have accumulated a foundation of life experiences and knowledge to draw from when learning
  • are goal oriented
  • are relevancy oriented
  • are practical and focused on how to apply new knowledge
  • expect to be shown respect
  • are motivated by a need to maintain or increase self-esteem
  • want to solve problems and apply new knowledge immediately

Instructors Should:

  • provide a structured syllabus
  • help learners integrate new ideas with what they already know
  • articulate their expectations, as well as understand student expectations
  • recognize the learner's need to control pace and length of lessons
  • take advantage of adult's preference to self-direct
  • remember that self-direction does not mean isolation
  • refrain from spoon-feeding
  • set a cooperative learning climate
  • create opportunities for mutual planning

Motivation

  • the reason that causes certain behavior
  • people will not typcially gain new knowledge or skills without motivation

Positive Motivation

Promise of reward

Positive Motivation is always more effective

  • secure, pleasant environment
  • tangible returns for efforts
  • group approval and belonging
  • favorable self-image

Negative Motivation

Also effective for a time, but overall detrimental to a learners continued growth.

Should be avoided (except possibly with extremely overconfident, impulsive, unsafe learners).

  • threats
  • fear
  • criticism

Barriers to Motivation

Loss of Motivation or Interest

Humans will inevitably experience drops in motivation, especially as novelty and excitement wears off.

Instructors can

  • praise incremental success
  • present new challenges
  • relate each block of learning to larger objectives
  • vary training
  • remind the learner of their goals and motivations

Learning Plateaus

Plateaus are normal and usually temporary.

Barriers to Motivation

Emotional Reactions

Anxiety and Stress
  • fear or nervousness about a potential outcome that hinders learning

  • may cause

    • appropriate, adequate, rapid response
    • freezing, resignation, incorrect responses
  • reinforce positive aspects of flying
  • reduce stress outside of flying
Impatience
  • learner seeks only the ultimate objective without proper consideration of the preliminary tasks
  • potential result of pacing set for a slower learner
  • instructors should present clear steps related to goals
Worry or Lack of Interest
  • can be caused by concern about or progress as well as concerns completely unrelated to training
  • divert focus to task at hand
  • ensure learner understands where they are in their progress and what deficiencies still exist
Physiological Factors
  • illness
  • airsickness
  • dehydration
  • extreme temperatures
  • poor ventilation

  • fatigue (acute and chronic)

    • inattention
    • distractibility
    • errors in timing
    • neglect of secondary tasks
    • loss of accuracy and control
    • lack of awareness of error accumulation
    • irritability
Apathy Due to Inadequate Instruction
  • lessons should be prepared in advance, appropriate to the learner, and contain accurate content
  • confidence in an instructor is difficult to regain

Barriers to Motivation

Defense Mechanisms

An unconscious mental process to protect oneself from axiety and unpleasant emotions that can distort, transform, or otherwise falsify reality.

  • Repression
    • placing uncomfortable thoughts into inaccessible areas of the unconsious mind
    • someone who is assaulted might not remember the event
  • Denial
    • refusing to accept external reality
    • heavy drinker says it is not affecting their health
  • Compensation
    • disguising a weak quality by emphasizing a strong one
    • student compensates for their harsh landings by emphasizing that their steep turns were in standards
  • Projection
    • relegating the blame for shortcomings onto other people or conditions
    • student blames their lack of ground knowledge on their instructor
  • Rationalization
    • justifying actions that otherwise would be unacceptable
    • student claims they failed an exam because there was not enough time
  • Reaction Formation
    • faking a belief that is opposite what the person actually feels
    • student claiming that they do not care if they fail thier check ride
  • Fantasy
    • daydreaming about how things should be rather than taking action
    • spending more time thinking about being an airline pilot than studying to become one
  • Displacement
    • shifting an emotion from the original cause to a less threatening substitute
    • upset about failing a checkride, a student lashes out at a friend

Communication

The effectiveness of communication is measured by the similarity between what the source intends and what the receiver receives.

The goal of communication is to cause a positive change in the behavior of the learner.

Source

Where the information comes from

  • must be able to adjust the symbols for the interpretation of the reveiver
  • information must be accurate, current, and stimulating

Symbols

How the information is encoded in a medium

  • differenct mediums might be appropriate for different ideas
  • ensure the learner understands and identifies with symbols that are used

Receiver

Where the information is interpreted

  • ensure understanding by having the learner repeat the information
  • positive change in behavior

Barriers to Effective Communication

Lack of Common Experience

  • words can mean different things to people based on different experience
  • ensure that terminology is understood

Confusion Between Symbol and the Symbolized Object

  • words can be confused with the intended representation

Overuse of Abstractions

  • an abstraction is a more general, vague way to refer to something
  • abstractions can cause confusion when they are not specific enough

External Factors

  • Physiological - hearing impairment
  • Environmental - radio static
  • Psychological - distrust

Developing Communication Skills

Role Playing

  • people play a specific role to create a practice environment
  • chair teaching

Instructional Communication

  • understand the concepts thoroughly
  • use examples and personal experiences
  • determine understanding using evaluation

Listening

  • instructors are not always the "Source" when communication is happening
  • effective communication is hearing WITH comprehension
  • listen to understand, not refute

Questioning

  • ensure understanding using frequent and brief questioning
  • ask open ended questions
  • encourage the learner to explaing and paraphrase

Instructional Enhancement

  • the better and deeper an instructor understands a topic, the better they can teach it

Instructor / Learner Relationship

The relationship that a student has with their instructor will directly influence their ability to learn.

  • everyone has a different learning style
  • good instructors develop multiple teaching styles to accomodate different learners
