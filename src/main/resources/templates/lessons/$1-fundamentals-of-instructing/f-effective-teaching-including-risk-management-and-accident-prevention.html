Risk Management

Defining Risk

Risk is the probability and possible severity of accident from exposure to various hazards.

Principles of Risk Management

  • Accept no unnecessary risk
  • Make risk decisions at the appropriate level
  • Accept risk when benefits outweigh the costs
  • Integrate risk management into planning at all levels

Risk Management Process

  • Identify the Hazard
  • Assess the Risk

Aeronautical Decision Making and Human Factors

Systematic approach to the mental process used to determine the best course of action

DECIDE Model

  • Detect the problem
  • Estimate the need to react
  • Consider all the alternatives
  • Identify the best alternatives
  • Do the necessary actions
  • Evaluate and monitor the effects

Factors That Inhibit ADM

  • Hazardous Attitudes
  • Distractions (losing situational awareness while teaching)
  • Complacency (overconfidence caused by repeated experience without incident)
  • Stress
  • Fatigue

Teaching Risk Management

When

  • From the beginning and throughout
  • Preliminary ground lesson about risk management before any flight time
  • Continuously integrated into the training
  • Particularly during every pre and post flight briefing

RM During Phases of Training

Different considerations during different phases of training.

  • Private Pilots

    • Pre-Solo - learner performs simple RM, uses non-numerical FRAT
    • Post-Solo - learner performs broader RM with guidance from instructor
    • XC - learner performs all RM with approval from instructor
  • Instrument Pilots - focus on complex weather and instrument failures
  • Transitioning Aircraft - focus on aspects of the new aircraft
  • Recurrent - use scenarios that mirror the pilot's typical environment
  • Professional Pilots - scale RM complexity to match the flight

Managing Risk During Flight Instruction

Using fundamental risk management, mitigate new risks introduced by the instruction environment.

Pilot

  • Aware of personal aeromedical conditions as well as those of the learner
  • Be prepared for learner's errors / lack of situational awareness
  • Select areas / altitudes appropriate for training
  • Clear transfer of controls

Aircraft

  • Instructor might not have access to trianer aircraft maintenance logs
  • Often flying small trainer aircraft with two pilots
  • Ensure learner performed a thorough preflight (oil, fuel)

Environment

  • Aware of changing airspace
  • Busy and crowded airspace or airports used for practice
  • Hearing radio calls while giving instruction
  • Ensure learner performed a thorough preflight (weather, notams)

External Pressures

  • Keeping pace of training
  • Completing lessons before deadlines or bad weather
  • Inadequate training because of financial constraints

Other Considerations

  • Always involve the learner in RM. Share responsibility for airworthiness, traffic avoidance, go / no go decisions.
  • Be familiar with the aircraft. Never teach in an unfamiliar aircraft.
  • Establish good habits through teaching but also by example.
  • Be adaptable. Coach the learner through your decision process as you make changes.
  • Expose the learner to all possible scenarios.

Considerations When Teaching Takeoffs and Landings

  • Teach when the learner is able to listen and absorb.
  • Avoid teaching mechanically. Instead identify problems and offer solutions in real time.
  • Be ready to take controls.
