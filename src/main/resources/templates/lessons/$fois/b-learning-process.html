Learning Process

Objective

  • Understand how adults acquire knowledge and skills.
  • Understand how memory works.
  • Apply educational theories to manage risks and provide effective flight instruction.

Topics

  • What is Learning

What is Learning?

A change in behavior as a result of experience.

Two Theories

Behaviorism
  • focuses on overt behavior
  • learning is a response to environmental stimuli (rewards and punishments)
  • Pavlov's dog
Cognitive Theory
  • focuses on what's happening inside the mind
  • learning is a process of thinking, knowing, and perceiving.

How We Construct Meaning

Perceptions

All learning comes from perceptions via our senses.

Pyramid of Needs

Insight

The result of grouping perceptions into meaningful wholes.

Acquiring Knowledge: The three phases:
  • memorization
  • understanding
  • application

The Laws and Domains of Learning

The 6 Laws of Learning (REEPIR)

  • Readiness: Students learn best when they are ready to learn.
  • Exercise: Things most often repeated are best remembered
  • Effect: Learning is strengthened by a positive emotional feeling
  • Primacy: What is learned first creates a strong impression (teach it right the first time!)
  • Intensity: A dramatic or exciting learning experience teaches more than a boring one
  • Recency: Things most recently learned are best remembered.
Domains of Learning (CAP) - Cognitive (Thinking): Knowledge, rote memory, and analysis - Affective (Feeling): Tying emotions, values, and safety attitudes to flying - Psychomotor (Doing): Physical hands-on stick-and-rudder skills

Skill Acquisition and Practice

Stages of Skill Acquisition

  • Cognitive Stage: Student memorizes steps
  • Associative Stage: Student practices and self-corrects
  • Automatic Response: Performance is smooth and requires little conscious thought

Learning Plateaus - Slumps in progress

CFI Tip: Mitigate by moving to a different maneuver or giving the student a break

Types of Practive

  • Deliberate: Aimed at a specific goal with feedback.
  • Blocked: Repeating the same drill over and over (leads to good short-term performance, poor long-term retention).
  • Random: Mixing up maneuvers (better for long-term retention).
Scenario-Based Training (SBT): Using highly realistic situations to teach decision-making rather than just isolated maneuvers.

Memory, Forgetting, and Retention

Three Chambers of Memory
  • Sensory: Receives input from senses, discards or passes it to STM in seconds.
  • Short-Term (STM): Holds information for about 30 seconds; limited capacity (approx. 7 items).
  • Long-Term (LTM): Permanent storage; requires coding and significance to access.
Why We Forget (RIFD):
Retrieval Failure, Interference, Fading/Decay, Depression/Repression
Retention of Learning: Praise, favorable attitudes, association, meaningful repetition, and multi-sensory learning help retain info
Transfer of Learning
Positive: Learning one thing helps learn another (e.g., driving a manual transmission helps understand taxiing engine control)
Negative: Learning one thing hinders another (e.g., steering a car with a steering wheel vs. steering a plane with rudders)
Assessment, Errors, and Workload Management
Evaluation vs. Critique: Evaluation tests the student against a standard (grading)
Critique provides immediate, useful feedback to improve
Slip: Plans to do the right thing, but does the wrong action
Mistake: Intends to do the wrong thing and succeeds (lack of knowledge)

Conclusion

Understanding HOW people learn makes you a capable teacher.
