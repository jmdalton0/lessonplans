Human Behavior

The study of human behavior is an attempt to explain how and why humans function the way they do.

Behavior is a combination of many factors, including innate human nature as well as individual experiences.

Satisfying Needs

Another definition of human behavior is the result of attempts to satisfy needs.

All humans have needs, ranging from simple (air, food, sleep) to complex (fulfilling relationships, reaching potential).

Instructor / Learner Relationship

Each learner has a different style of learning.

Instructors must be able to adapt to different learning styles.

Changing Instructors

Not everyone will meld.

Make sure your student knows they can change instructors if needed.

Motivation and Human Needs

Motivation is the reason that causes someone to behave a certain way.

A person will not typcially gain new knowledge or skills without motivation.

Positive Motivation

Promise of reward

Positive Motivation is always more effective

  • secure, pleasant environment
  • tangible returns for efforts
  • group approval and belonging
  • favorable self-image

Negative Motivation

Also effective for a time, but overall detrimental to a learners continued growth.

Should be avoided (except possibly with extremely overconfident, impulsive, unsafe learners).

  • threats
  • fear
  • criticism

Maintaining Motivation

Humans will inevitably experience drops in motivation, especially as novelty and excitement wears off.

As instructors, we can:

  • praise incremental success
  • relate each block of learning to larger objectives
  • vary training
  • remind the learner of their goals and motivations

Plateaus are normal and usually temporary.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Higher levels are more complex.

To an extent, humans cannot satisfy a need on one level until the needs on all the lower levels are met.

  • Physiological
  • Safety and Security
  • Belonging
    • give and recieve friendship and love
  • Esteem
    • Internal - self-esttem, confidence, independence
    • External - recognition, reputation, status
  • Cognitive and Aesthetic - understand the world around you
    • Cognitive - knowledge, understanding
    • Aesthetic - opinions, tastes (can affect instructor / learner relationship)
  • Self-Actualization
    • fulfill potential
    • solve problems
    • concerned about personal growth
    • appreciate life

Defense Mechanisms

Unconscious mental process to protect oneself from axiety and unpleasant emotions.

Defense Mechanisms tend to distort, transform, or otherwise falsify reality

  • Repression

    Placing uncomfortable thoughts into inaccessible areas of the unconsious mind.

    Example: some who is assaulted might not remember the event

  • Denial

    A refusal to accept external reality.

    Example: heavy drinker says it is not affecting their health

  • Compensation

    Disguising a weak quality by emphasizing a strong one.

    Example: student compensates for their harsh landings by emphasizing that their steep turns were in standards

  • Projection

    Relegating the blame for shortcomings onto other people or conditions.

    Example: student blames their lack of ground knowledge on their instructor

  • Rationalization

    Justifying actions that otherwise would be unacceptable.

    Example: student claims they failed an exam because there was not enough time

  • Reaction Formation

    Faking a belief that is opposite what the person actually feels.

    Example: student claiming that they do not care if they are a difficult personality to fly with

  • Fantasy

    Daydreaming about how things should be rather than taking action.

    Example: spending more time thinking about being an airline pilot than studying to become one

  • Displacement

    Shifting an emotion from the original cause to a less threatening substitute.

    Example: upset about failing a checkride, a student lashes out at a friend
