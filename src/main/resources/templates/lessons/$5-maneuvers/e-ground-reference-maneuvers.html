Normal Landing

Procedure

Soft Field Landing

Purpose

Runway conditions are not always hard, dry asphalt. Runways might have a surface that is dirt, sand, grass, snow, etc.

  • allow the wings to hold more of the aircrafts weight
  • reduce stress on the nose gear
  • reduce chances of getting stuck

Procedures

Similar to a normal landing, however

  • maintain back pressure to keep the nose wheel off of the ground
  • keep nose wheel off the ground until loss of elevator authority
  • minimize braking while taxiing off the runway

Common Errors

  • applying too much back pressure and lifting off again
  • allowing the nose wheel to touch down prematurely
  • applying brakes prematurely

Short Field Landing

Purpose

To clear obstacles and land on shorter runways.

  • increase approach angle
  • minimize ground roll on landing
  • clear obstacles in vicinity of airport

Procedure

Similar to a normal landing, however

  • lower approach speed
  • higher approach angle

Common Errors

  • too fast on approach
  • too low on approach
  • too much flare causing floating
  • too much brakes
  • failing to apply maximum aerodynamic braking

Power Off 180 Landing

Purpose

To simulate an engine failure in the pattern.

To practice precise energy management when landing.

Procedure

Similar to a normal landing, however

  • pull power idle when abeam your landing spot (do not adjust again unless unsafe)
  • pitch for Vbg
  • modify turns as necessary for wind, altitude, obstructions, etc.

ACS Standards

  • touch down on specified point or within 200 feet
  • proper pitch attitude
  • no side drift
  • longitudinal axis aligned with and over runway centerline

Techniques

  • Base turn is decided by wind and altitude. A strong headwind or low altitude will require a shorter final leg.
  • Do not lower full flaps until landing is assured.
  • Be aware that without power, lowering full flaps will cause an initial float, then an agressive loss of altitude.

Common Errors

  • failing to specify a touch down point before beginning
  • failing to be configured properly in downwind (appropriate airspeed and altitude)
  • lowering full flaps too early
  • raising flaps after they have been lowered

Risk Management

Similar to a short field landing, pilots may become fixated on hitting their spot.

Be aware of the following potential hazards.

  • Uncoordinated turns. Do not risk spinning.
  • Unstable approach. Go around if neccessary.
  • Raising flaps at low altitudes. This causes an abrupt loss of altitude.
  • Harsh landings. Firm is acceptable. Establish proper pitch attitude when touching down.

Crosswind Corrections

Side Slip

  • ailerons into wind
  • align nose with centerline using rudder

Crab

  • rudder into wind
  • wings level

Other Wind Considerations

Tailwind Landing

Increases landing distance.

Corrections
  • land with a headwind
  • go around
  • be aware of increased landing distance

Wind Shear

Any abrupt change in wind direction or speed.

Corrections
  • increase approach airspeed
  • add half the gust factor
  • go around

Conclusion

  • verify aircraft and engine performance
